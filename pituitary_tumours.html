Pituitary Tumour (pit-yoo-it-ary tyoo-ma)

A pituitary tumour is a type of non-cancerous brain tumour that grows on a pea-sized structure at the base of the brain called the pituiatry gland.

How does it feel?

The symptoms caused by a pituitary tumour usually develop gradually. The most common symptoms is loss of vision (in particular peripheral vision). Less commonly, symptoms of a pituitary tumour can be due to hormone imbalance.
Symptoms due to hormone imbalance can be due to an excess or deficiency of hormones produced by the pituitary gland. These can include generalised weakness, loss of menstrual periods, sexual dysfunction, increased urination, or unintended weight loss of gain.

What can we do?

If your doctor thinks you may have a pituitary, you'll be referred to our clinic for further tests, such as an MRI or CT scan and hormone tests. Treatment depends on the size and position of your tumour, how fast it's growing and your general health.
We will discuss the options at your appointment which might include monitoring with regular brain scans, surgery to remove the tumour and radiosurgery. Surgery to remove a pituitary tumour can often be carried out using keyhole surgery in which the tumour is removed through one or both nostrils. All these options carry some risks and some benefits and we encourage you to talk openly about your wishes so that we can come to a shared plan at your appointment.

What about the future?

You'll need to have regular scans after treatment to check if the tumour is growing again. Most tumours grow very slowly and those that grow quickly are usually treated before they become too big.
If growth happens quickly and remains untreated, however, the tumour can affect the nerve to the eye and cause loss of vision. Even with treatment, impaired vision can persist and affect your ability to work, communicate or drive. We can offer more information and support to manage your symptoms at your appointment. There are a number of research trials underway to try and improve the treatment of pituitary tumours, and you may be asked if you wish to take part in research at your appointment.
